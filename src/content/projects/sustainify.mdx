Sustainify is a Chrome extension that scans a product page while you're shopping and returns a sustainability score with a short justification. The score is built from signals inspired by the ESG framework — material composition, transport emissions, ethical sourcing, community approval — and sits alongside a small list of greener alternatives surfaced from sustainability-focused subreddits.

Beyond scoring individual products, the extension keeps a personal Stash of things you've bought. Each item contributes to an overall Sustainify Score broken down by category, so you can see where your footprint is heaviest. A small plant on your dashboard grows or wilts with your score — a light gamified layer to make the feedback loop feel less clinical.

## How it's built

The frontend is framework-less — plain JavaScript, HTML, and CSS — which made it harder to build but left us with a light, modular extension that's easy to move around. We used `chrome.storage` for persistence and syncing, so flipping themes or adding to your Stash in one surface updates every other surface instantly. The backend is three Gumloop workflows: one for detecting product pages, one for scoring, one for pulling alternatives. We call them concurrently to keep the user-visible latency low.

## What I worked through

The hardest call was the alternative-products feature. Searching the open web was slow and often drifted off-topic; maintaining a manual database didn't scale to the breadth of products we wanted to cover. We landed on Reddit as a stopgap — constraining the AI to sustainability-minded subreddits cut hallucinated links and kept suggestions grounded.

The other thread was being deliberate about *when* to use an LLM. With abundant AI access it's easy to delegate everything, but that's both slow and — ironically, given the project — wasteful. We kept pushing logic back into plain JS where we could (e.g. a DFS over the page DOM to extract text before any AI call), and only reached for LLMs where structured reasoning was actually needed.

## What's next

The open problem is giving sustainability scores to *every* product online in a way that's defensible. ESG plus our own research got us to a reasonable estimate, but there's no canonical metric here — the next step would be talking to people who work on this professionally and tightening the methodology before scaling further.
